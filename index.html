Internet History, technology and Security

Created by: University of Michigan

When Late 2012
Total TimeSpent 30 hrs
in 2.5 months
Completed
Platform Coursera
Institution Un of Michigan
Homepage Go !
Official description

The impact of technology and networks on our lives, culture, and society continues to increase. The very fact that you can take this course from anywhere in the world requires a technological infrastructure that was designed, engineered, and built over the past sixty years. To function in an information-centric world, we need to understand the workings of network technology. This course will open up the Internet and show you how it was created, who created it and how it works. Along the way we will meet many of the innovators who developed the Internet and Web technologies that we use today.

Why I started this course ?

1) After playing with different online learning platforms (Apple university, Stanford online) without never really engaging with the material, I discoreved Coursera. I wanted to try this new (back in 2012) learning format that looked very engaging. This was my first MOOC enrolement and the first MOOC I've finished.
2) The second reason is because I love the Internet, and I always wanted to know how it came to be.

Key takeaways

With this course, I understood the underlaying history and structures that made/make internet possible. Turing's enigma machines @ Bletchley Park to be the grand parent of computers. Then a network of academics that becomes ARPANET leading Sir Tim Berners-Lee to create the world wide web in CERN. On the "technology" part of the course, four-layer model, HTTP as an application layer, TCP/IP, all those concepts were aligned in order to undertand the foundations of the internet. Lastly, in the "security" I've learned that ...

As my first experience with MOOCs, I was fascinated. Finally a real way to learn online !

Internet security Ignorant: Secure > Unsecure Novice: First approach to digital signatures, SSL, encoding and encryptation.

Official description

The Data Analyst Nanodegree program is specifically designed to prepare you for a career in Data Science. As a Data Analyst, you are responsible for obtaining, analyzing, and effectively reporting on data insights ranging from business metrics to user behavior and product performance. This program’s curriculum was developed with leading industry partners to ensure students master the most cutting-edge curriculum. Graduates will emerge fully prepared for this amazing career.

Why I started this course ?

I always was intrigued by information transactions. Since I discovered the internet in 1995, I was hooked by its potential as the information broadcasting network that it is. In the early 2000's, data minning was the edge skill to have, but I lacked the programming skills to enter the environment. Fast forward 10 years, with idustrial engineering studies, 5 years of digital marketing experience and a couple of MOOCs later (ex: PY4E), I wanted to 'enter' the data science ecosytem. During 2016, top influencers [1] [2] started debating about our IA future. I wanted to build an opinion and grow the skills to be an actor in this information rich reality.

Key takeaways

Take aways from this ~ 500 hrs degree are plenty, but there will be divided in 2 sections, a) Data Sciences b) Project based long commitment MOOC's .

  1. Understating of Data Sciences :
    • Different tasks require different roles in the data universe: Data Analyst, Data Engineer and Data Scientist.
    • What's relationship between Artifial Intelligence, Deep learning and Machine Learning, data analysis, business anlaysis and how it relates to business decisions
    • What's a suitable framework in order to apply data analysis to business : 1. Questions, 2. Data wrangling, 3. Data exploration, 4. Conclusions, 5. Vizualisation/Communication
  2. Project based long commitment MOOC's :
    • This is definitely a more enriching way to be immersed in the material (compared to lecture based short commitment courses). Project means having a clear guideline and having to deliver confined by those guidelines. Community discussions, tutoring, multiple submissions and corrections are the best ways of learning.
    • At the end of the course, projects delivered for course completion becomes a portfolio (cf SYLLABUS & PROJECTS section)
    • Delivering a project demand much more commitment and active understanding than watching/reading material.
    • Personnaly, I will challenge myself with more project based online courses from now on pursuing my life long learning journey :)
    • Udacity platform and their "Nanodegree" [1] is a perfect example of this type of proposition.

Social Network Analysis

Created by: University of Michigan

When Late 2013
Total TimeSpent 45 hrs
in 8 weeks
Completed
Platform Coursera
Institution University of Michigan
Homepage Go ! (N/A)
Official description

Everything is connected: people, information, events and places, all the more so with the advent of online social media. A practical way of making sense of the tangle of connections is to analyze them as networks. In this course you will learn about the structure and evolution of networks, drawing on knowledge from disciplines as diverse as sociology, mathematics, computer science, economics, and physics. Online interactive demonstrations and hands-on analysis of real-world data sets will focus on a range of tasks: from identifying important nodes in the network, to detecting communities, to tracing information diffusion and opinion formation.

Why I started this course ?

After my engineering masters thesis based on Social network in corporate environment [1], and my position in a digital marketing department, I was hooked by social networks. Facebook platform in particular.
Aiming for a cost effective framework to foster virality (2013's buzzword) in marketing campaigns, I took this course in order to add technicality to our paid display campaigns.

Key takeaways

The main takeaway for this course was that social networks can be essentially a math described phenomena.
I also found that concepts explained in lecture had too much depth for an introductory course (cf. syllabus) reaching the limits of what pure online MOOC's can do in less than 10 weeks.
Lastly, it's because of the optional (programming) material I could not to play with that I decided to take a programming MOOC afterwards.

Skill improvement

Before After
Gephi Ignorant: Downloaded once in highschool for trigonometry homework, never used. Novice: Concluded basic social network analysis and visualization with Gephi
Social Network Analysis Ignorant: Social Network analysis is helpfull to conduct analysis to social networks (duh) Novice: Familiarity with core descriptives properties of networks: betweenness, closeness, centrality, etc.

Syllabus [1] & projects

Highlight readings

Week 1: What are networks and what use is it to study them?
Concepts: nodes, edges, adjacency matrix, one and two-mode networks, node degree Activity: Upload a social network (e.g. your Facebook social network into Gephi and visualize it ).
Week 2: Random network models: Erdos-Renyi and Barabasi-Albert
Concepts: connected components, giant component, average shortest path, diameter, breadth-first search, preferential attachment Activities: Create random networks, calculate component distribution, average shortest path, evaluate impact of structure on ability of information to diffuse
Week 3: Network centrality
Concepts: betweenness, closeness, eigenvector centrality (+ PageRank), network centralization Activities: calculate and interpret node centrality for real-world networks (your Facebook graph, the Enron corporate email network, Twitter networks, etc.)
Week 4: Community
Concepts: clustering, community structure, modularity, overlapping communities Activities: detect and interpret disjoint and overlapping communities in a variety of networks (scientific collaborations, political blogs, cooking ingredients, etc.)
Week 5: Small world network models, optimization, strategic network formation and search
Concepts: small worlds, geographic networks, decentralized search Activity: Evaluate whether several real-world networks exhibit small world properties, simulate decentralized search on different topologies, evaluate effect of small-world topology on information diffusion.
Week 6: Contagion, opinion formation, coordination and cooperation
Concepts: simple contagion, threshold models, opinion formation Activity: Evaluate via simulation the impact of network structure on the above processes
Week 7: Cool and unusual applications of SNA
Hidalgo et al. : Predicting economic development using product space networks (which countries produce which products) Ahn et al., and Teng et al.: Learning about cooking from ingredient and flavor networks Lusseau et al.: Social networks of dolphins Activity: hands-on exploration of these networks using concepts learned earlier in the course
Week 8: SNA and online social networks
Concepts: how services such as Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter, CouchSurfing, etc. are using SNA to understand their users and improve their functionality Activity: read recent research by and based on these services and learn how SNA concepts were applied

I've gained perspective to understand also that corporate leaders may not have the experience to understand how quick and disruptive digital wave is, as young professionals might not have the experience of being in charge of an "it's already working" machine.

Understanding Media by Understanding Google

Created by: Northwestern University

When Late 2014
Total TimeSpent 30 hrs
in 6 weeks
Completed
Platform Coursera
Institution Northwestern University
Homepage Go !
Official description

Google Inc. is one of the key success stories of the Internet era. The company has expanded beyond its original search business through innovation and acquisition to touch the lives of nearly every person who lives life online. For example, Americans spend more than 3,400 hours per year using consumer media, the field where Google’s impact is most profound, and citizens around the world must understand what the company has wrought not only to control their offline and online environments, but also to interact and engage successfully with anyone in our professional and personal lives.

Enrollees in this course learn how to understand the tactics that modern media companies, journalists, marketers, politicians, technologists, and social networks are using to reach them and affect their behavior. They learn how to adopt strategies that put them on an even footing with these entities in achieving their own communications goals. They 

  • think about, react to, and write about excerpts from half a dozen important books; 
  • read a sampling of newspaper and magazine reportage from Google’s entire history;
  • monitor news sites and specialized blogs about the company and its competitors while the course is in session;
  • take note of their own usage of Google and other online resources; and
  • learn how to anticipate the future impact of the company and its competitors on aspects of the media such as information consumption, creation, and distribution. 

Why I started this course ?

Mid 2014 and 2 years in my position of CRM manager at Renault Marketing, I was fasinated by digital marketing. I had some heated exchanges with digital/media agency planners (Havas media) on how performance marketing could be improved.
Google and Facebook were raising as the only two options for a "let's not throw money" campaign (as is the case today in mid 2017 [1]). So I took this course to have a broader undertanding of what was "media" and how digital companies are disrupting it.

Key takeaways

Main takeaways of this reading oriented course is the opinion that we are entering the attention economy. Our attention is being commoditised by disruptive tech/media companies (ex: freemium ad based digital services).
This shift as an impact on our society as information looses value compared to attention demanding data [1]. It is our responsibility to take actions about this.

I believe advertising has become a tax that the poor and the technologically illiterate pay

Prof. Scott Galloway, NYU business school professor and L2 founder Source
